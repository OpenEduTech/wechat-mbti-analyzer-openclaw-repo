# Report Patterns

## Default Personal Report

Use this structure for one-to-one chats:

1. Analysis scope and method
2. Executive summary
3. Why the subject looks like a given type
4. Four-dimension analysis
5. Relationship or collaboration pattern
6. Main strengths
7. Main friction points
8. Practical suggestions
9. Final conclusion

## Default Group Report

Use this structure for group chats:

1. Analysis scope and caveats
2. Executive summary
3. Group operating model
4. Member-by-member role analysis
5. Likely MBTI tendency for each person
6. Team strengths
7. Team risks
8. Structural recommendations
9. Final conclusion

## Wording Rules

- Prefer "more like" and "closer to" framing over absolute labels.
- Explicitly state uncertainty.
- Separate role behavior from stable personality when needed.
- Treat MBTI as a chat-based inference, not a diagnosis.

## Helpful Chinese Phrases

Use these ideas when writing the final Chinese report:

- This looks more like a work-context personality expression.
- This is not a formal test result, but a behavior-based inference from chat records.
- This tendency becomes clearer under stress.
- The real issue may be role boundaries and expectation mismatch, not personality incompatibility.
- The key is not who is right or wrong, but how each side understands the other's battlefield.

## Comparison Framing

For multi-person comparison, ask:

- who defines direction
- who compresses options
- who stabilizes reality
- who implements details
- who brings outside signals

Only map likely MBTI after the functional role is clear.
